The New Wave of Tbilisi Restaurants You Need to Know

February 18, 2025 6 min read By Nino Kapanadze

Georgia's capital is experiencing a culinary renaissance that feels both inevitable and surprising. In a city where grandmothers still roll khinkali by hand and corner bakeries pump out shotis puri from clay ovens at dawn, a new generation of chefs is rewriting the rules — not by abandoning tradition, but by building on it with fearless creativity.

Walk through the winding streets of Vera or the revitalized courtyards of Old Town, and you'll find restaurants that didn't exist two years ago. They're intimate, often tucked behind unmarked doors or hidden in renovated Soviet-era apartments. The menus are short, seasonal, and unapologetically Georgian — but with a twist that speaks to global influence without losing local soul.

Where Tradition Meets the Unexpected

The shift started quietly. Around 2022, a handful of young chefs who had trained in Copenhagen, Barcelona, and Tokyo began returning to Tbilisi. They didn't want to open fine-dining temples. Instead, they gravitated toward the city's raw, unpolished spaces — converting garages, basements, and even a former printing press into restaurants that feel more like dinner parties than establishments.

Take Shavi Lomi, which has long been the standard-bearer for modern Georgian cuisine. But the newer wave goes further. At places like Café Littera and the newer Tabla, chefs are deconstructing classics — turning ajapsandali into a cold-pressed terrine, or serving churchkhela as a dessert course with walnut ice cream and grape reduction.

The Natural Wine Connection

You can't talk about Tbilisi's food scene without talking about wine. Georgia's 8,000-year winemaking tradition has become the secret engine of its restaurant boom. Natural wine bars have multiplied across the city, and many of the best new restaurants double as wine bars, curating lists that showcase small-production qvevri wines from Kakheti, Kartli, and Imereti.

"In Tbilisi, wine isn't a pairing — it's a conversation. Every bottle has a story, and every winemaker is someone's cousin."

Venues like Vino Underground and g.Vino pioneered this movement, but the latest crop pushes further into experimentation. Wine flights are served alongside tasting menus that evolve with the harvest. It's an approach that feels simultaneously ancient and cutting-edge.

Where to Go

  • Café Littera — Set in the Writers' House courtyard, this is Georgian fine dining at its most elegant. The garden terrace in summer is unmissable.
  • Tabla — A quieter, more experimental space in Vera. The seasonal tasting menu changes weekly and sources exclusively from Georgian farms.
  • Shavi Lomi — The original modern Georgian restaurant. Still relevant, still surprising. Their pork belly with tkemali glaze is legendary.
  • Barbarestan — Based on a 19th-century Georgian cookbook, every dish here is a history lesson. The presentation is modern; the recipes are 150 years old.
  • Kakhelebi — For those who want traditional Kakhetian hospitality with a contemporary touch. The wine list alone is worth the visit.

The Neighborhood Effect

What makes Tbilisi's food scene unique is its intimacy. This isn't a sprawling metropolis where trends happen in isolation. When a new restaurant opens in Sololaki, the chef from Vera comes for dinner. The sommelier from Old Town drops by with a bottle. There's a collaborative spirit that bigger cities have lost — or never had.

This interconnectedness means quality rises quickly. A great ingredient — say, a particular farmer's tomatoes from Lagodekhi — will appear on three menus within a week. Not because of competition, but because chefs share discoveries with genuine excitement.

For travelers, this creates an experience that's hard to find elsewhere. You're not just eating at restaurants — you're plugging into a living network of taste, tradition, and creative ambition. And the best part? In Tbilisi, a world-class meal with a bottle of natural wine still costs less than an appetizer in most European capitals.

The new wave isn't replacing the old. It's extending it. And for anyone who loves food, Georgia's capital has never been more exciting.
